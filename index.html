HOW IT GREW

A small story, one season at a time.

1975

My Father's Field

Srinivas, my father, plants his first crop. Two cows in the shed. A well, a wooden gate, and the patience of someone who loved the soil.

2014

A Boy at the Gate

A child arrived one evening with nothing. We made a bed. We made another the next week. By winter, the spare room was full and a new room had to be built.

2019

An Elder at the Door

An old man, left at a bus stand by people who could not care for him anymore. He stayed two years. Others followed. We named the place The Butterfly House.

Today

A Whole, Quiet Place

Twelve children. Nine elders. A few cows, a kitchen garden, a fruit grove. Whatever we don't eat, we give to the village. Whatever the village can spare, they give back.

A Letter from the Founder

Why we built this place

This is not a big dairy. We have a few cows. Some mornings we get six gallons. Some mornings four. We drink what our home needs and the rest goes to the children and elders who live here.

I built The Butterfly House on the land my father Srinivas farms with me. Not alone — with him, with neighbors, with friends, with families who heard a child needed a place to sleep and brought a mattress, who heard an old woman had nowhere and brought a chair.

We grow vegetables and fruits in the garden behind the house. Whatever the kitchen does not use, the village comes for. Whatever the village can spare, it sends back. That is how a small farm feeds more than its own table.

— with care

The Founder

Built alongside my father, Srinivas — who still walks the fields every morning

A Quiet Home · For Two Kinds of Wings

The Butterfly House

A small home for abandoned children and forgotten elders.
Twelve children. Nine elders. One family.

Some butterflies are just learning to fly. Others have flown a long, long way. Both deserve a window, a meal, and someone who will listen. We try to be that house.

Things People Ask Us

Honest Answers

A small place, often misunderstood. Here is what we are and what we are not.

Are you an orphanage? An old-age home?

expand_more

Both. Neither. We are a family that grew. The children and elders share a kitchen, a garden, and most evenings on the verandah. Whatever you want to call it, we just call it home.

Do you sell the milk and vegetables?

expand_more

No. The kitchen takes what it needs and the rest goes to the village. People bring back what they can. Some days that is rice, some days a hand to weed, some days nothing — and that is also fine.

How do donations get used?

expand_more

School fees, books, uniforms, doctor's visits, medicine for the elders, repairs to the roof when the monsoon finds a hole. We keep a ledger pinned in the kitchen that anyone can read.

Can I volunteer? What is needed?

expand_more

Yes — teachers for the children, doctors and nurses for the elders, hands in the garden, drivers for the cart, and anyone willing to sit and listen to a long story patiently. Tell us what you can spare and we will tell you when.

Can I bring my children to visit?

expand_more

Saturday mornings, please. Tea on the verandah, a tour of the garden, and the elders will likely steal them for stories. Send us a note first — the gate is small.

Why call it the Butterfly House?

expand_more

The first child we took in saw a butterfly on the gate the morning he arrived. He told an elder that the butterfly had brought him here. We did not have a better story than his, so we used it.
